*EEOC Issues Report on Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) in the Federa=
l Sector*

Results Show Federal Agencies Usually Offered ADR During EEO Counseling, =
But 40% of ADR Policies Were Incomplete

WASHINGTON =96 The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to=
day issued a research report,=A0Alternative Dispute Resolution in the Fed=
eral Sector [ https://www.eeoc.gov/federal-sector/reports/alternative-dis=
pute-resolution-federal-sector?utm_content=3D&utm_medium=3Demail&utm_name=
=3D&utm_source=3Dgovdelivery&utm_term=3D ],=A0which provides comprehensiv=
e information and data on the effectiveness of federal Alternative Disput=
e Resolution (ADR) programs and their practices in fiscal year (FY) 2019.=
 The report sampled 24 federal agencies through conducting focus groups a=
nd a survey of equal employment opportunity (EEO) directors and ADR progr=
am managers.

=93Alternate dispute resolution remains a vital option in EEO process,=94=
 said Dexter Brooks, associate director of the EEOC=92s Office of Federal=
 Operations. =93Mutually acceptable resolution creates an opportunity for=
 the parties to find a way forward. This report highlights areas for ADR =
programs to continue improvement of operations.=94

Main findings from this report include:


  * Approximately 40% of agencies=92 ADR policies were incomplete in FY 2=
019. Failing to state the timeline involved in the ADR process was the mo=
st common policy deficiency discovered during a review of agencies=92 pol=
icies.=20
  * There were approximately 30,029 completed informal resolution efforts=
, or counselings, with the vast majority (27,492) offering ADR. Data vari=
ed by agency size, however.=20
  * During the formal complaint phase, which begins with the filing of an=
 official complaint of harassment or discrimination, the average offer to=
 participate in ADR at large agencies (35.4%) was more than double the of=
fer rate at small (12.5%) and midsize (8.7%) agencies. This may reflect t=
he fact that larger agencies tend to have more resources available.=20
  * The settlement rate during the formal complaint phase (37.9%) was mor=
e than double the rate during the informal counseling phase (16.1%). This=
 suggests that agencies were more willing to negotiate when an employee f=
iles a formal complaint.=20
  * The average withdrawal rate was 24.6% during the counseling phase, co=
mpared to 6.7% during the formal phase.=20

Survey results showed that agencies typically updated their ADR policies =
and procedures every three years. A third of agencies did not conduct reg=
ular self-assessments of their program=92s effective=ADness. Nearly a qua=
rter of agencies did not annually evaluate their programs at all.

Focus groups revealed that little information existed on the correlation =
between participation in ADR and employee retention rates. Agencies had n=
ot tracked the turnover of complainants following their participation in =
ADR.

Agencies often did not offer ADR in these instances:


  * 60% of agencies did not provide ADR in sexual harassment complaints a=
nd roughly 80% of agencies did not offer ADR for other reasons, which inc=
luded complaints regarding selections, criminal history, and medical and =
security clearance issues.=20
  * ADR is generally not offered when the complaint came from a non-emplo=
yee (e.g., applicant or contractor).=20

Furthermore, certain agency actions may delegitimize the ADR program in t=
he eyes of complainants. Of the agencies surveyed:


  * Nearly 19% of agencies allowed the manager accused in the complaint t=
o be the settlement authority.=20
  * Nearly a quarter of agencies did not require managers and management =
officials to participate in ADR.=20
  * A majority of agencies, about 80%, did not routinely provide annual, =
agencywide ADR training, and approximately 70% of agencies did not provid=
e annual ADR training to agency leaders.=20

One positive finding is that the vast majority of agencies surveyed (95%)=
 stated that they ensured that the settlement authority was available dur=
ing active ADR sessions.

<p style=3D"text-align: center; line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-fa=
mily: helvetica; font-size: 14px; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"center"><=
strong>EEOC Issues Report on Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) in the =
Federal Sector</strong></p>
<p style=3D"text-align: center; line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-fa=
mily: helvetica; font-size: 14px; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"center">R=
esults Show Federal Agencies Usually Offered ADR During EEO Counseling, B=
ut 40% of ADR Policies Were Incomplete</p>
<p style=3D"text-align: left; line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-fami=
ly: helvetica; font-size: 14px; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"left"><br>W=
ASHINGTON =96 The U.S. Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) tod=
ay issued a research report,=A0<a href=3D"https://lnks.gd/l/eyJhbGciOiJIU=
zI1NiJ9.eyJidWxsZXRpbl9saW5rX2lkIjoxMDEsInVyaSI6ImJwMjpjbGljayIsInVybCI6I=

kaXVtPWVtYWlsJnV0bV9uYW1lPSZ1dG1fc291cmNlPWdvdmRlbGl2ZXJ5JnV0bV90ZXJtPSIs=
ImJ1bGxldGluX2lkIjoiMjAyMzAzMjguNzQxNjcxMDEifQ.PNivss_ZFgyhYm__l8mAtp6Nqs=

"noopener" style=3D"color: #336699; text-decoration: underline; -ms-word-=
break: break-all; word-break: break-word; -webkit-hyphens: none; -moz-hyp=
hens: none; hyphens: none;">Alternative Dispute Resolution in the Federal=
 Sector</a>,=A0which provides comprehensive information and data on the e=
ffectiveness of federal Alternative Dispute Resolution (ADR) programs and=
 their practices in fiscal year (FY) 2019. The report sampled 24 federal =
agencies through conducting focus groups and a survey of equal employment=
 opportunity (EEO) directors and ADR program managers.</p>
<p style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fon=
t-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"left">=93Al=
ternate dispute resolution remains a vital option in EEO process,=94 said=
 Dexter Brooks, associate director of the EEOC=92s Office of Federal Oper=
ations. =93Mutually acceptable resolution creates an opportunity for the =
parties to find a way forward. This report highlights areas for ADR progr=
ams to continue improvement of operations.=94</p>
<p style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fon=
t-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"left">Main =
findings from this report include:</p>
<ul>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">Approximately 40% of a=
gencies=92 ADR policies were incomplete in FY 2019. Failing to state the =
timeline involved in the ADR process was the most common policy deficienc=
y discovered during a review of agencies=92 policies.</li>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">There were approximate=
ly 30,029 completed informal resolution efforts, or counselings, with the=
 vast majority (27,492) offering ADR. Data varied by agency size, however=
.</li>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">During the formal comp=
laint phase, which begins with the filing of an official complaint of har=
assment or discrimination, the average offer to participate in ADR at lar=
ge agencies (35.4%) was more than double the offer rate at small (12.5%) =
and midsize (8.7%) agencies. This may reflect the fact that larger agenci=
es tend to have more resources available.</li>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">The settlement rate du=
ring the formal complaint phase (37.9%) was more than double the rate dur=
ing the informal counseling phase (16.1%). This suggests that agencies we=
re more willing to negotiate when an employee files a formal complaint.</=
li>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">The average withdrawal=
 rate was 24.6% during the counseling phase, compared to 6.7% during the =
formal phase.</li>
</ul>
<p style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fon=
t-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"left">Surve=
y results showed that agencies typically updated their ADR policies and p=
rocedures every three years. A third of agencies did not conduct regular =
self-assessments of their program=92s effective=ADness. Nearly a quarter =
of agencies did not annually evaluate their programs at all.</p>
<p style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fon=
t-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"left">Focus=
 groups revealed that little information existed on the correlation betwe=
en participation in ADR and employee retention rates. Agencies had not tr=
acked the turnover of complainants following their participation in ADR.<=
/p>
<p style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fon=
t-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"left">Agenc=
ies often did not offer ADR in these instances:</p>
<ul>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">60% of agencies did no=
t provide ADR in sexual harassment complaints and roughly 80% of agencies=
 did not offer ADR for other reasons, which included complaints regarding=
 selections, criminal history, and medical and security clearance issues.=
</li>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">ADR is generally not o=
ffered when the complaint came from a non-employee (e.g., applicant or co=
ntractor).</li>
</ul>
<p style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fon=
t-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"left">Furth=
ermore, certain agency actions may delegitimize the ADR program in the ey=
es of complainants. Of the agencies surveyed:</p>
<ul>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">Nearly 19% of agencies=
 allowed the manager accused in the complaint to be the settlement author=
ity.</li>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">Nearly a quarter of ag=
encies did not require managers and management officials to participate i=
n ADR.</li>
<li style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fo=
nt-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 0 0 7px;">A majority of agencies=
, about 80%, did not routinely provide annual, agencywide ADR training, a=
nd approximately 70% of agencies did not provide annual ADR training to a=
gency leaders.</li>
</ul>
<p style=3D"line-height: 1.4; color: #000000; font-family: helvetica; fon=
t-size: 14px; text-align: left; margin: 5px 0 10px;" align=3D"left">One p=
ositive finding is that the vast majority of agencies surveyed (95%) stat=
ed that they ensured that the settlement authority was available during a=
ctive ADR sessions.</p>
